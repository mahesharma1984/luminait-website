Sample Analysis Guide

This is what your child learns to see in a text — not plot summary, but the underlying pattern that makes essays possible.

Example: Romeo and Juliet by William Shakespeare

1

The Pattern

What this teaches
We don't list themes. We show your child the organising principle — the single idea that makes everything else make sense. Once they see the pattern, they can explain why every detail matters.

The Dread Engine

Shakespeare tells us Romeo and Juliet will die before we even meet them. The Prologue announces: "A pair of star-crossed lovers take their life."

This creates dread, not suspense. We don't wonder IF they'll die — we know they will. We watch them fall in love knowing it will destroy them.

Every technique Shakespeare uses — foreshadowing, dramatic irony, light/dark imagery — serves this pattern. The play is an engine designed to make us feel helpless as we watch hope move toward doom.

2

The Devices

What this teaches
These aren't random literary terms. Each device is selected because it creates the pattern. Your child learns to trace how techniques work together to produce meaning.
  • Dramatic Irony
    The audience knows what characters don't. Every love scene is shadowed by our knowledge of death.
  • Foreshadowing
    Characters predict their fate without realising it. Their words condemn them before the events unfold.
  • Light/Dark Imagery
    Light begins as love, but inverts — daylight becomes danger, darkness becomes safety. The imagery tracks hope becoming tragedy.
JULIET (Act 1, Scene 5)
"My grave is like to be my wedding bed."
Juliet means: "If he's married, I'll die unmarried." But we hear the literal truth she doesn't know.
3

The Timeline

What this teaches
Students learn to see the shape of the text — how meaning builds across the whole work, not just isolated moments. This is what transforms spotting devices into writing essays.
Prologue
Death announced. Dread established. Audience knows the ending.
Acts 1-2
Love blooms in shadow. Light = hope. Every romantic moment shadowed by our foreknowledge.
Act 3
The turn. Mercutio dies, Romeo kills Tybalt, hope collapses. Light inverts — dawn means Romeo must leave.
Acts 4-5
Descent into tragedy. The plan fails. The deaths we knew were coming finally arrive.
4

The Conflicts

What this teaches
The tensions that drive essay questions. Your child learns to identify the arguments the text is making — not just "themes exist" but "here's what the author is saying about them."
Montagues vs Capulets
The ancient feud. Hatred inherited, not chosen. The lovers are doomed by a conflict they didn't create.
Fate vs Free Will
Are they "star-crossed" (destined) or destroyed by choices? The play holds both possibilities in tension.
Love vs Society
Private feeling against public duty. Their love is real, but the world won't allow it.
Youth vs Age
The parents' feud kills the children. The old world destroys the new.
5

Essay Toolkit

What this teaches
The bridge to writing. Understanding the text is step one — we show them how to turn that understanding into an essay. This is where comprehension becomes argument.

Sample Thesis

Romeo and Juliet uses dramatic irony and foreshadowing to position the audience as helpless witnesses to a tragedy we cannot prevent, creating dread rather than suspense.

Essay Approach

Each body paragraph takes one device and shows how it creates the "dread engine" pattern:

  • Paragraph 1: Dramatic irony — the gap between audience knowledge and character hope
  • Paragraph 2: Foreshadowing — characters predicting their own deaths
  • Paragraph 3: Light/dark imagery — hope transforming into danger
